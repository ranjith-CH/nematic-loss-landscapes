Abstract

We propose a geometric framework for neural network loss landscapes in which the leading Hessian eigenvector is treated as a nematic director evolving along the gradient descent trajectory. The Frank elastic energy of this field — measured by the accumulated director angular velocity \(\Omega(t)\) — predicts gradient instabilities during training \((r = 0.905,\; p = 2.8\times10^{-30})\). The spectral gap \(\Delta\lambda = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2\) measures director field stiffness and correlates with training difficulty \((r = 0.789,\; p = 1.5\times10^{-43})\). Adam reduces accumulated Frank energy by 47% relative to SGD. At the grokking transition in a transformer trained on modular arithmetic, \(\Omega\) peaks \(4.9\times\) baseline, \(\Delta\lambda\) spikes then collapses 81%, and sharpness \(\lambda_1\) drops 40% simultaneously — demonstrating that behavioural transitions produce measurable geometric signatures.

Framework

At each training step \(t\) the network occupies a point \(\theta(t) \in \mathbb{R}^N\). The local loss landscape around that point is characterised to second order by the Hessian:

\[ H(\theta(t)) \;=\; \nabla^2 L(\theta(t)) \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}, \quad \text{symmetric} \]

Definition 1 — Nematic Director

The nematic director is the leading Hessian eigenvector, normalised:

\[ \mathbf{n}(t) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{v}_1(\theta(t))}{\|\mathbf{v}_1(\theta(t))\|} \;\in\; S^{N-1} \] \[ \text{where}\quad H(\theta(t))\,\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_1(t)\,\mathbf{v}_1, \qquad \lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_N \] The identification \(\mathbf{n} \equiv -\mathbf{n}\) holds automatically — eigenvectors are headless. This is the nematic symmetry, without any additional assumption.

Assumption 1 — Slow Landscape Variation

We assume the loss landscape geometry varies slowly relative to the training step size:

\[ \|\theta(t) - \theta(t-1)\| \;\ll\; \ell_{\mathbf{n}} \] where \(\ell_{\mathbf{n}}\) is the correlation length of the eigenvector field. Under this condition, \(\Omega(t)\) measures genuine geometric evolution rather than sampling artefacts.

The assumption holds for typical learning rates and is violated near instabilities — precisely when \(\Omega(t)\) spikes. The spikes are therefore a signal of genuine rapid geometric change, not noise.

Definition 2 — Angular Velocity \(\Omega(t)\)

Measures how fast the principal curvature direction rotates between consecutive steps:

\[ \Omega(t) \;=\; 1 - \bigl|\langle \mathbf{n}(t),\, \mathbf{n}(t-1) \rangle\bigr| \;\in\; [0,\,1] \] \(\Omega = 0\): director unchanged    \(\Omega = 1\): director has rotated \(90^\circ\)

Definition 3 — Spectral Gap \(\Delta\lambda(t)\)

Measures how well-defined the director is at each step:

\[ \Delta\lambda(t) \;=\; \lambda_1(t) - \lambda_2(t) \;\geq\; 0 \] Large \(\Delta\lambda\): one dominant curvature direction, director sharp.
Small \(\Delta\lambda\): two nearly equal eigenvalues — director ambiguous, landscape locally isotropic.

Definition 4 — Accumulated Frank Energy \(F(t)\)

The Frank elastic energy accumulated along the training trajectory:

\[ F(t) \;=\; \sum_{s=0}^{t} \Omega(s)^2 \] Analogue of \(\int |\nabla \mathbf{n}|^2\,dx\) in liquid crystal theory, restricted to the gradient descent trajectory. High \(F(t)\) indicates geometrically disordered training.

Scope of the analogy

We measure \(F(t)\) along the training trajectory, not over all of \(\mathbb{R}^N\). No claims are made about global topology, topological defects, or winding numbers. The identification \(\mathbf{n} \equiv -\mathbf{n}\) is exact and requires no additional assumption.

Results

E1 — \(\Omega(t)\) predicts gradient instabilities

Director angular velocity predicts subsequent gradient norm spikes across training runs on CIFAR-10.

\[ r = 0.905, \quad p = 2.8\times10^{-30}, \quad n = 300 \text{ epochs} \]

E2 — Frank energy correlates with spectral gap compression

In the high-difficulty regime, \(F(t)\) correlates with \(1/\Delta\lambda(t)\).

\[ r = 0.789, \quad p = 1.5\times10^{-43} \quad \text{(hard task regime)} \]

E3 — Adam reduces Frank energy vs SGD

Under matched effective learning rates, Adam reduces \(F(T)\) by 47%.

\[ \Delta F(T) = -47\%, \quad p < 0.05 \quad \text{(matched step size)} \]

E4 — Grokking: three simultaneous geometric signals

Transformer on \((a+b) \bmod 97\), \(P = 14{,}785\). At generalisation transition, epoch 1220:

\[\Omega:\; 4.9\times \text{ baseline mean} \quad (0.061 \text{ vs } 0.012)\] \[\Delta\lambda:\; \text{spikes to } 1859\text{, collapses } {-81\%} \quad (\to 358)\] \[\lambda_1:\; \text{drops } 40\% \quad (6615 \to 3982)\] All three predicted before running the experiment.
Secondary events: epoch 1900 (\(3.5\times\)), epoch 2400 (\(9.5\times\), EOS at \(\lambda_1 \to 2/\eta\)).
Limitations

Trajectory restriction

\(F(t)\) is measured along the gradient descent trajectory, not over \(\mathbb{R}^N\). Global field structure is not characterised.

No topological claims

Topological defects and winding numbers are not computed or claimed.

Post-grokking variance

\(\Omega\) and \(\Delta\lambda\) distributions are wider after the grokking transition due to secondary events at epochs 1900 and 2400.

Scale

Experiments use small-to-medium models. Scaling to large models requires randomised SVD and is left to future work.
