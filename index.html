Abstract

We propose a geometric framework for neural network loss landscapes in which the leading Hessian eigenvector is treated as a nematic director evolving along the gradient descent trajectory. The Frank elastic energy of this field — measured by the accumulated director angular velocity Ω(t) — predicts gradient instabilities during training (r = 0.905, p = 2.8×10⁻³⁰). The spectral gap Δλ = λ₁ − λ₂ measures director field stiffness and correlates with training difficulty (r = 0.789, p = 1.5×10⁻⁴³). Adam reduces accumulated Frank energy by 47% relative to SGD, providing a geometric explanation for its stability advantage. At the grokking transition in a transformer trained on modular arithmetic, Ω peaks 4.9× baseline, Δλ spikes then collapses 81%, and sharpness λ₁ drops 40% simultaneously — demonstrating that behavioural transitions produce measurable geometric signatures. We propose Ω(t) and Δλ as lightweight monitoring signals for training instability detection.

Framework

At each training step t, the network sits at parameter point θ(t) ∈ ℝᴺ. The local loss landscape L(θ(t) + δ) is characterised to second order by the Hessian H(θ(t)) = ∇²L(θ(t)). We define the nematic director as the leading Hessian eigenvector:

n(t) = v₁(θ(t)) / ‖v₁(θ(t))‖ ∈ Sᴺ⁻¹ Note: n ≡ −n (eigenvectors are headless — exact nematic symmetry)

The director captures the principal curvature direction of the local landscape. We characterise its dynamics through two scalar quantities:

Ω(t) = 1 − |⟨n(t), n(t−1)⟩| [angular velocity, ∈ [0,1]] Δλ(t) = λ₁(t) − λ₂(t) [spectral gap, ≥ 0] F(t) = Σₛ₌₀ᵗ Ω(s)² [accumulated Frank energy]

High Ω(t) indicates the local landscape's principal curvature direction is rotating rapidly — the optimizer is navigating geometrically disordered terrain. Low Δλ(t) indicates the director is poorly defined (two nearly equal eigenvalues), making the landscape nearly isotropic locally. Both signal reduced training stability.

Results

  • E1 Ω(t) predicts gradient instabilities. Across training runs on CIFAR-10 with varying learning rates, director angular velocity Ω(t) predicts subsequent gradient norm spikes. r = 0.905, p = 2.8×10⁻³⁰
  • E2 Frank energy correlates with spectral gap compression. In the high-difficulty training regime, accumulated Frank energy F(t) correlates with 1/Δλ(t) — confirming the geometric interpretation. r = 0.789, p = 1.5×10⁻⁴³
  • E3 Adam damps director rotation relative to SGD. Under matched effective learning rates, Adam reduces accumulated Frank energy by 47% compared to SGD — a geometric explanation for Adam's empirical stability advantage. 47% reduction, p < 0.05
  • E4 Grokking produces a three-signal geometric event. At the generalisation transition (epoch 1220) in a transformer trained on modular arithmetic (a+b mod 97), three simultaneous signatures appear: Ω peaks 4.9× baseline mean, Δλ spikes to 1859 then collapses 81%, and λ₁ drops 40%. These were predicted before running the experiment. Ω = 4.9×, Δλ collapse = 81%, λ₁ drop = 40%

Limitations

Trajectory-restricted measurement. We measure the Frank energy along the gradient descent trajectory, not over the full parameter space. This is the natural observable from a single training run but does not capture the global field structure.
No topological claims. We do not claim topological defects or compute winding numbers. The nematic analogy is used only for the director dynamics and Frank energy — not for topological invariants.
Violin plots post-grokking. The distribution of Ω and Δλ is wider after the grokking transition, not narrower as a simple stability argument would predict. Secondary events at epochs 1900 and 2400 contribute to this variance.
Scale. Experiments use small-to-medium models. Whether the Frank energy signal scales to large language models requires randomised SVD approximations and is left to future work.
